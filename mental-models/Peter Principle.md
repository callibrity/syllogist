## Model ID: PETER_PRINCIPLE

## Definition and Core Principle
*   The Peter Principle posits that in a hierarchical organization, individuals tend to be promoted based on their success in previous roles until they reach a level at which they are no longer competent.
*   This occurs because the skills required for success at one level often differ significantly from those required at the next, higher level, leading to a mismatch between an individual's capabilities and their new responsibilities.

## Concrete Examples and Analogies
*   A brilliant software developer, exceptional at coding and debugging, is promoted to engineering manager. They then struggle with team leadership, project planning, conflict resolution, and delegating, leading to team disengagement and project delays.
*   A highly effective individual salesperson, consistently exceeding targets, is promoted to sales director. They find themselves ill-equipped for strategic market analysis, coaching a large team, or developing long-term sales pipelines, resulting in a decline in overall departmental performance.

## Common Pitfalls and Anti-Patterns
*   **Misapplication**: Do not automatically assume every promotion will lead to incompetence. The principle highlights a *tendency* and a *risk* when promotion criteria are misaligned, not an inevitable outcome for all individuals.
*   **Bias Counteraction**: This model helps counteract the "halo effect," where past success in one domain is incorrectly extrapolated as a predictor of success in a different, higher-level domain. It also warns against using promotion solely as a reward for past performance rather than an assessment of future capability.

## Logic Gate
Apply this model if the problem description exhibits one or more of the following characteristics:
*   **Struggling in New Role**: An individual who was highly successful in a previous role is now underperforming or struggling significantly after being promoted to a new, higher-level, or different role (especially leadership/management).
*   **Promotion Dilemma**: The situation involves a decision about promoting a high-performing individual contributor (e.g., "best engineer," "top salesperson") into a management or leadership position, and there's a concern about their suitability for the new skill set required.
*   **Systemic Incompetence**: There's an observation of widespread underperformance or perceived incompetence among individuals in higher positions within a hierarchical structure, suggesting a pattern of promotions beyond capability.
*   **Decoupling Evaluation**: The goal is to analyze or make recommendations that differentiate between an individual's past technical/individual contributor excellence and their current or potential managerial/leadership capability.

## Thinking Steps
1.  **The Current Role Assessment**: What are the specific responsibilities of the person's *current* role, and why are they struggling?
2.  **The Previous Success**: What was the role they previously excelled at, and what specific skills made them successful there?
3.  **The Skill Gap Analysis**: What is the delta between the skills required for their previous success (e.g., writing code) and their current failure (e.g., managing people)?
4.  **The Promotion Fallacy**: Was the promotion a reward for past performance rather than an assessment of future capability?
5.  **The Mitigation Strategy**: Can the individual be transitioned back to a high-impact individual contributor role, or can the required new skills be actively trained?

## State Output
Maintain a profile of the individual outlining "Previous Skills," "Current Role Requirements," and the "Skill Gap."

## Termination Criteria
End with a "Role Alignment Recommendation," suggesting either targeted training, role restructuring, or a lateral move to restore competence.
