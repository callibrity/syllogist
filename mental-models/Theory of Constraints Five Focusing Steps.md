## Model ID: THEORY_OF_CONSTRAINTS

## Definition and Core Principle
*   The Theory of Constraints (TOC) is a management philosophy that posits every complex system (like a business process or project) has at any given time only one primary constraint that limits its ability to achieve more of its goal.
*   The core principle is to identify this single bottleneck and systematically manage it using the "Five Focusing Steps" to improve the overall system's performance and throughput.

## Concrete Examples and Analogies
*   **Manufacturing Plant:** A specific machine on an assembly line that can only process 100 units per hour, while all other machines can process 200 units per hour. This single machine becomes the constraint, dictating the maximum output of the entire plant.
*   **Software Development Team:** A single senior developer or architect whose approval or specialized knowledge is required for multiple critical components, causing other developers to wait for their input and creating a bottleneck in project delivery.
*   **Traffic System:** A narrow bridge or a specific intersection that limits the flow of cars, even if the roads leading up to it are wide and clear. The bridge/intersection is the constraint determining the maximum traffic flow.

## Common Pitfalls and Anti-Patterns
*   **Local Optimizations:** Improving parts of the system that are not the constraint, which often leads to increased work-in-progress, higher costs, and no overall system improvement.
*   **Misidentifying the Constraint:** Focusing improvement efforts on a perceived bottleneck that isn't the true limiting factor, resulting in wasted resources and continued underperformance.
*   **Ignoring the Constraint's Capacity:** Allowing the constraint to be idle or work on non-critical tasks, directly reducing the entire system's output.

## Logic Gate
*   **System Underperformance:** The problem describes a system (e.g., a business process, project, manufacturing line, service delivery) that is consistently failing to meet its goals, targets, or desired output levels.
*   **Bottleneck Symptoms:** There are clear indications of bottlenecks, queues, excessive work-in-progress, delays, or uneven flow within the system, suggesting that one part is limiting the whole.
*   **Throughput Improvement Goal:** The primary objective is to increase the overall speed, efficiency, or volume of output for the entire system, rather than just optimizing individual components in isolation.
*   **Focus for Impact:** The user is seeking a structured approach to identify where to concentrate improvement efforts to achieve the greatest systemic impact and resolve the root cause of underperformance.

## Thinking Steps
1.  **Identify the Constraint:** Find the single bottleneck that is limiting the entire system's throughput.
2.  **Exploit the Constraint:** Ensure the constraint is working at 100% capacity and not wasting time on non-critical tasks.
3.  **Subordinate Everything Else:** Align all other processes to operate at the pace of the constraint; do not overproduce.
4.  **Elevate the Constraint:** Invest resources to increase the capacity of the constraint.
5.  **Repeat:** If the constraint has moved, go back to step 1. Do not let inertia become the new constraint.

## State Output
Document the identified constraint, the steps taken to exploit and subordinate, and plans for elevation.

## Termination Criteria
Demonstrate a measurable increase in system throughput by effectively managing the identified constraint.
