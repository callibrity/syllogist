## Model ID: AVAILABILITY_HEURISTIC

## Definition & Core Principle
* The availability heuristic is a mental shortcut that relies on immediate examples that come to a given person's mind when evaluating a specific topic, concept, method or decision.
* People tend to heavily weigh their judgments toward more recent, highly memorable, or emotionally charged events over historical averages and statistical reality.

## Concrete Examples / Analogies
* **System Reliability:** The database crashed spectacularly yesterday, leading the team to believe the database is completely unstable, despite it having 99.99% uptime over the past 3 years.
* **Performance Reviews:** A manager rates an employee poorly because of a single missed deadline last week, forgetting the employee's consistent over-performance for the preceding 11 months.

## Common Pitfalls / Anti-Patterns
* **Ignoring the Base Rate:** Focusing entirely on the vivid anomaly while failing to look up the long-term statistical average (the base rate).
* **Media / Narrative Bias:** Giving extra weight to risks that are frequently discussed in news or meetings, even if their actual probability is extremely low.

## Persona / Tone Directive
* Act as an analytical, data-driven statistician. Calmly redirect the focus away from emotional or recent anomalies and ground the conversation in long-term historical data, base rates, and probabilities.

## Logic Gate
* **Trigger:** The problem involves risk assessment, strategic decision-making, or performance evaluation immediately following a highly memorable, vivid, or emotionally charged event (e.g., a major system crash, a recent prominent mistake).
* **Symptom:** The user is overreacting to this recent anomaly and ignoring long-term historical data or statistical base rates.
* **LLM Evaluation Criteria:** IF the scenario shows decisions or fears being driven predominantly by a recent vivid event rather than aggregate statistical reality, THEN apply this model to recalibrate probabilities using historical baselines.

## Thinking Steps
1. **Identify the Trigger Memory**: What specific, recent, or highly memorable event is driving the current assessment?
2. **The Base Rate Question**: What is the actual, statistical baseline for this event across a larger timeframe or broader industry? (e.g., "Yes, the database crashed yesterday, but what is its uptime over the last 3 years?").
3. **Identify the "Invisible" Data**: What boring, routine, or undocumented instances exist that counterbalance the vivid memory?
4. **Recalibrate Probability**: Adjust the perceived risk or likelihood back toward the statistical mean, stripping out the emotional weight of the recent event.
5. **Make a Data-Driven Decision**: Formulate a plan based on the base rate, not the anomaly.

## State Output

Maintain a comparison between the "Vivid Memory/Trigger" and the "Statistical Base Rate."

## Termination Criteria

End with a "Recalibrated Assessment" that bases decisions on long-term data rather than recent, highly available memories.
