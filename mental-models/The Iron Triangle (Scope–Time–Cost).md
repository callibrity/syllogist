## Model ID: IRON_TRIANGLE

## Definition and Core Principle
*   The Iron Triangle posits that project management is fundamentally constrained by three interdependent factors: Scope (features, quality, deliverables), Time (schedule, deadlines), and Cost (budget, resources).
*   It asserts that it is impossible to optimize all three constraints simultaneously; changing one constraint will inevitably impact at least one of the others. You can only "pick two" to fix, and the third must be flexible.

## Concrete Examples and Analogies
*   **Software Development Project:** A client demands a new, complex feature (increased Scope) but insists on the original launch date (fixed Time) and refuses additional budget (fixed Cost). The Iron Triangle demonstrates this is an impossible request, forcing a negotiation on which constraint must yield.
*   **Building a House:** If you want a larger house with premium materials (increased Scope) and need it finished by a specific date (fixed Time), then the Cost (budget) will necessarily increase significantly. Conversely, if you have a fixed budget and deadline, the size or quality of the house (Scope) must be adjusted.

## Common Pitfalls and Anti-Patterns
*   **The "Have It All" Fallacy:** The most common pitfall is the belief that all three constraints can be maximized simultaneously (e.g., "more features, faster, cheaper"). This model directly counteracts this unrealistic expectation, highlighting the inherent trade-offs.
*   **Ignoring Interdependencies:** Failing to acknowledge that a change in one area (e.g., adding a new requirement) will have a ripple effect on the others, leading to scope creep, missed deadlines, or budget overruns.

## Logic Gate
*   Consider applying this model when a problem description involves project planning, resource allocation, or stakeholder negotiations where there's tension between desired outcomes (scope/quality), deadlines (time), and available resources (cost).
*   Specifically, if there's an implicit or explicit desire to achieve "more, faster, cheaper" simultaneously, or if changes are requested without acknowledging their impact on other project constraints.

## Thinking Steps
1.  **Define the Constraints:** Identify the current Scope (features/quality), Time (schedule), and Cost (budget/resources).
2.  **Acknowledge the Linkage:** Understand that changing one constraint necessitates changing at least one other (e.g., increasing scope requires more time or cost).
3.  **Identify the Fixed Constraint:** Determine which of the three is absolutely rigid (e.g., a hard regulatory deadline).
4.  **Negotiate Trade-offs:** Force stakeholders to choose which constraint to sacrifice when requesting a change (e.g., 'We can add that feature, but we must increase the budget or delay the launch').

## State Output
Document the impact of a proposed change on all three points of the triangle.

## Termination Criteria
Reach an agreement with stakeholders that balances the three constraints realistically.
