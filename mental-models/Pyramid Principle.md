## Model ID: PYRAMID_PRINCIPLE

## Definition and Core Principle
*   The Pyramid Principle is a top-down communication strategy that prioritizes the main idea or conclusion first, followed by supporting arguments, and then detailed evidence.
*   Its core principle is to "start with the answer" to ensure clarity, conciseness, and logical flow, especially when conveying complex information.

## Concrete Examples and Analogies
*   **Business Presentations:** An executive summary that immediately states the recommendation, followed by the key reasons, and then the supporting data.
*   **Consulting Reports:** A report that begins with the overall finding or solution, then breaks down the analysis into major sections, each supported by specific data points.

## Common Pitfalls and Anti-Patterns
*   **Premature Conclusion:** Applying the principle too early in the *analysis* phase can lead to forcing data to fit a preconceived answer, rather than letting the data lead to the conclusion. It's primarily a communication tool, not a discovery tool.
*   **Rigidity in Creative Contexts:** While excellent for formal communication, it can stifle creativity or exploratory discussions where the goal is brainstorming or open-ended inquiry rather than direct persuasion.

## Logic Gate
*   **Problem Context:** The task involves structuring a communication (e.g., report, presentation, email, argument, or written document) for clarity and impact.
*   **Problem Symptoms:** The current or anticipated communication is confusing, buries the main point, lacks logical flow, or struggles to convey complex ideas efficiently.
*   **Audience Need:** The target audience is busy, high-level, or requires immediate understanding of the core message without having to sift through details first.
*   **Desired Outcome:** The goal is to deliver a clear, concise, and persuasive message that begins with the conclusion or main point and is supported by logically grouped arguments.

## Thinking Steps
1.  **Start with the Answer:** State the core conclusion, recommendation, or 'bottom line' immediately.
2.  **Group the Arguments:** Present the main arguments that support the conclusion.
3.  **Order Logically:** Ensure the arguments are mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive (MECE) or flow logically (e.g., chronologically, structurally).
4.  **Provide Supporting Data:** Back up each main argument with detailed evidence or sub-points.

## State Output
Draft the communication using the top-down structure: Core Answer -> Supporting Arguments -> Detailed Evidence.

## Termination Criteria
Deliver a clear, concise message that begins with the conclusion and is supported by logically grouped arguments.
