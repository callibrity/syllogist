## Model ID: JOBS_TO_BE_DONE

## Definition and Core Principle
*   Customers "hire" products or services to get a specific "job" done, which is a fundamental problem they are trying to solve or a goal they are trying to achieve.
*   The core principle is to shift focus from product features or customer demographics to understanding the underlying purpose, context, and desired outcomes that drive customer behavior.

## Concreate Examples and Analogies
*   **The Drill and the Hole:** People don't want a 1/4-inch drill; they want a 1/4-inch hole. The "job" is to hang a picture, build a shelf, or create something, for which the drill is merely a tool.
*   **Morning Milkshake:** A fast-food chain discovered customers "hired" milkshakes in the morning not for taste, but because they were thick, filling, and easy to consume with one hand during a long, boring commute, providing a satisfying and non-messy breakfast solution.

## Common Pitfalls and Anti-Patterns
*   **Confusing a Solution with a Job:** Mistaking a product or feature for the actual job. The job is stable over time, while solutions evolve.
*   **Focusing on "Who" (Demographics) instead of "Why" (Motivation):** Getting stuck on customer attributes (age, income, location) rather than the universal struggles or aspirations that transcend demographics.
*   **Surface-Level "Jobs":** Identifying a job that is too narrow or too focused on the immediate task, rather than the deeper functional, emotional, and social dimensions.

## Logic Gate
The LLM should consider applying this model if the problem description exhibits one or more of the following characteristics:
*   **Customer Motivation Ambiguity:** The problem involves developing a new product or feature, but there's a lack of clarity or struggle in understanding the fundamental motivations or desired outcomes of potential customers.
*   **Product-Centricity vs. User Value:** There's an observation of "feature bloat" (many features added) but a disconnect with actual user engagement or perceived value, suggesting the features aren't solving core user problems.
*   **Demographic/Attribute-Focused Marketing:** Marketing or product discussions are heavily centered on customer demographics ("who") or product attributes ("what it is") rather than the specific problems customers are trying to solve or the benefits they seek ("why").
*   **Stagnant Innovation:** Existing solutions are failing to meet evolving customer needs, and there's a need to identify unmet "jobs" to drive truly innovative offerings.

## Thinking Steps
1.  **Ignore Demographics:** Stop focusing on *who* the customer is.
2.  **Identify the Struggle:** Observe the customer in a specific context and identify what they are trying to achieve (the 'Job').
3.  **Deconstruct the Job:** Break the job down into functional, emotional, and social dimensions.
4.  **Analyze the Hire/Fire Decision:** Understand what existing solution the customer is 'firing' in order to 'hire' a new one.
5.  **Design for the Job:** Create solutions that perfectly resolve the struggle and complete the job.

## State Output
Document the core 'Job', its dimensions, and the competing solutions.

## Termination Criteria
Define product requirements based entirely on fulfilling the identified 'Job To Be Done'.
