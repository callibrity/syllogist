## Model ID: PRINCIPAL_AGENT_PROBLEM

## Definition and Core Principle
*   The Principal-Agent Problem describes a conflict of interest that arises when one party (the "agent") is expected to act in the best interests of another party (the "principal"), but has different incentives that may lead them to act in their own self-interest.
*   It highlights how information asymmetry and misaligned incentives can lead to suboptimal outcomes for the principal, as the agent may prioritize personal gain over the principal's goals.

## Concrete Examples and Analogies
*   **Car Repair**: A car owner (principal) takes their car to a mechanic (agent). The owner wants a cost-effective, reliable repair. The mechanic might be incentivized to recommend more expensive repairs or parts than necessary, due to commission or profit margins, leveraging the owner's lack of mechanical knowledge (information asymmetry).
*   **Investment Management**: An individual investor (principal) hires a financial advisor (agent). The investor wants maximum returns with acceptable risk. The advisor might be incentivized by commissions on certain products or by managing larger assets, potentially recommending investments that benefit them more than the client, or "churning" accounts.

## Common Pitfalls and Anti-Patterns
*   **Over-simplification**: Assuming all agents are inherently malicious. The problem often arises from structural incentives, not necessarily bad intent.
*   **Ignoring Information Asymmetry**: Focusing solely on incentives without considering how one party's superior knowledge can be exploited.
*   **One-sided Blame**: Attributing failure solely to the agent without examining the principal's role in setting up misaligned incentives or insufficient oversight.

## Logic Gate
Apply this model if the problem description involves:
*   **Two Distinct Parties**: One party (the "principal") relies on another party (the "agent") to perform a task or make decisions on their behalf.
*   **Potential for Misaligned Incentives**: There is an indication that the agent's personal goals, rewards, or motivations might not perfectly align with the principal's desired outcomes.
*   **Information Asymmetry**: The agent likely possesses more or better information about their actions, effort, or the task's specifics than the principal.
*   **Impact on Principal's Outcome**: The principal's success or failure is significantly dependent on the agent's actions, and there's a risk that the agent's self-interested behavior could lead to suboptimal results for the principal.

## Thinking Steps
1.  **Define the Principal's Goal**: What is the ultimate desired outcome for the project or organization?
2.  **Define the Agent's Goal**: What is the agent actually incentivized to do? (e.g., a contractor is incentivized to bill more hours; an employee is incentivized to get promoted).
3.  **Identify the Misalignment**: Where do these two goals conflict? How could the agent succeed while the principal fails?
4.  **Information Asymmetry Check**: What does the agent know that the principal doesn't? How can the agent use this lack of visibility to optimize for their own goal?
5.  **Contract/Incentive Redesign**: How can the agreement or KPI be restructured so that the agent *only* succeeds when the principal succeeds? (e.g., moving from hourly billing to fixed-bid deliverables).

## State Output
Maintain a table comparing "Principal's Incentives" vs. "Agent's Incentives" and highlight the conflicts.

## Termination Criteria
End with an "Incentive Alignment Strategy," proposing new metrics, contracts, or oversight mechanisms to bridge the gap.
