## Model ID: SOCIO_TECHNICAL

## Definition and Core Principle
*   Socio-technical systems theory posits that organizations are complex systems where technical and social aspects are interdependent and must be jointly optimized for effective performance.
*   It emphasizes that changes in the technical system (e.g., tools, processes, architecture) inevitably impact the social system (e.g., people, relationships, culture) and vice versa, requiring a holistic approach to design and problem-solving.

## Concrete Examples and Analogies
*   **New Software Rollout:** Implementing a new enterprise resource planning (ERP) system (technical) without adequate user training, addressing resistance to change, or aligning incentives for adoption (social) often leads to low utilization, workarounds, and project failure.
*   **Agile Transformation:** Adopting Agile methodologies (technical process change) without fostering a culture of psychological safety, cross-functional collaboration, and empowered teams (social) can result in "fake Agile" where ceremonies are followed but true benefits are not realized.

## Common Pitfalls and Anti-Patterns
*   **Technocentric Bias:** The most common pitfall is focusing exclusively on the technical solution's elegance, efficiency, or features, while neglecting its impact on human behavior, motivation, organizational structure, or culture. This often leads to technically sound but socially unworkable solutions.
*   **Ignoring Interdependencies:** Treating technical and social problems as separate and solvable in isolation, rather than recognizing their deep interconnections and designing interventions that address both concurrently.

## Logic Gate
The LLM should consider applying the SOCIO_TECHNICAL model when:
*   A problem description involves the design, implementation, or analysis of systems where technology, processes, and human elements interact within an organizational context.
*   There are explicit or implicit indications of friction, inefficiency, or failure arising from a disconnect between technical solutions and human factors, team dynamics, or organizational culture.
*   The problem highlights both technical components (e.g., software, infrastructure, workflows, tools) and social components (e.g., user adoption, team collaboration, communication patterns, resistance to change, organizational structure, incentives, culture).
*   The objective is to achieve optimal outcomes by jointly considering and optimizing both the technical efficiency and the human experience, rather than prioritizing one over the other.

## Thinking Steps
1.  **Analyze the Technical System:** Understand the tools, processes, and architecture.
2.  **Analyze the Social System:** Understand the people, relationships, culture, and incentives.
3.  **Identify Interdependencies:** Map how changes in the technical system impact the social system and vice versa.
4.  **Joint Optimization:** Design solutions that optimize both the technical efficiency and the human experience, avoiding the sub-optimization of one for the other.

## State Output
Provide an analysis detailing the interplay between the technical architecture and the organizational culture, highlighting areas of friction.

## Termination Criteria
Propose an intervention that addresses both human workflows and technical implementation concurrently.
