## Model ID: LINDY_EFFECT

## Definition and Core Principle
*   The Lindy Effect posits that for certain non-perishable items, their future life expectancy is proportional to their current age. The longer something has survived, the longer it is likely to continue to survive.
*   It suggests that time acts as a filter: things that have been around for a long time have proven their resilience, utility, and adaptability, making them a statistically safer bet for future longevity.

## Concreate Examples and Analogies
*   **Technology Protocols:** Fundamental internet protocols like TCP/IP, HTTP, and SQL have existed for decades. Their continued widespread use and evolution, despite numerous technological shifts, exemplify the Lindy Effect in action, suggesting their enduring relevance.
*   **Literary Classics:** Books such as Shakespeare's plays or Homer's epics have survived centuries, indicating their timeless appeal and profound insights. Their longevity suggests they will continue to be read and studied for many more generations.

## Common Pitfalls and Anti-Patterns
*   **Applying to Perishable Items:** The Lindy Effect *only* applies to non-perishable things (ideas, technologies, books, protocols, institutions). It does *not* apply to perishable items like living organisms, news cycles, or fads, where age often correlates with proximity to an end. Misapplying it to perishable items will lead to incorrect conclusions.
*   **Ignoring Disruptive Innovation:** While powerful, the Lindy Effect is a probabilistic heuristic, not a guarantee. It doesn't account for sudden, truly disruptive paradigm shifts that could render even long-standing items obsolete (e.g., a completely new computing paradigm or scientific discovery). It should be used as one input among others, not the sole determinant.

## Logic Gate
*   **Problem Context:** The user is evaluating the long-term viability, stability, or future relevance of a non-perishable entity (e.g., a technology, idea, protocol, business strategy, or cultural artifact).
*   **Decision Point:** The user needs to make a choice between options with varying ages or track records, particularly when considering adoption, investment, or long-term commitment.
*   **Core Question:** The user is implicitly or explicitly asking, "How likely is this to last?" or "Is this a safe long-term bet?"
*   **Keywords:** Look for terms like "longevity," "sustainability," "future-proof," "risk of obsolescence," "track record," "proven," "new vs. old," "trend adoption," "library choice," "framework selection," "business strategy."

## Thinking Steps
1.  **Age Identification:** How long has this technology or idea been in continuous use? (e.g., "SQL has been around for 50 years; HTMX for 4 years").
2.  **Survival Metric:** Has it survived "extinction events" (e.g., the rise of NoSQL, the deprecation of similar libraries)?
3.  **Probability Projection:** Apply the Lindy Rule: The expected future life is proportional to the current age. (A 10-year-old library is likely to be relevant for 10 more years; a 6-month-old library is a gamble).
4.  **Fragility Check:** Is this item "perishable" (like a news cycle) or "non-perishable" (like a mathematical principle or a fundamental protocol)? The Lindy Effect only applies to non-perishables.
5.  **The "Consultant's Bet":** Should we bet the client's infrastructure on this? Compare the Lindy score of the "New/Shiny" vs. the "Old/Reliable."

## State Output

Structure the output as a "Longevity Forecast."

## Termination Criteria

End with a "Sustainability Score" from 1-10, where 1 is "High Risk of Obsolescence" and 10 is "Generational Standard."
