## Model ID: STAKEHOLDER_SALIENCE

## Definition and Core Principle
*   A framework for identifying and prioritizing stakeholders based on their attributes of Power (ability to influence), Legitimacy (validity of their claim), and Urgency (criticality and time-sensitivity of their claims).
*   The core principle is that stakeholders possessing more of these attributes demand greater attention and strategic engagement, allowing for efficient resource allocation and conflict management.

## Concrete Examples and Analogies
*   **Software Development Project:** When launching a new software product, stakeholders include users (high legitimacy, high urgency for bug fixes), investors (high power, high legitimacy for ROI), and the development team (high legitimacy, high urgency for technical decisions). This model helps prioritize addressing critical user feedback versus investor demands.
*   **Urban Planning Initiative:** In planning a new city park, stakeholders might include local residents (high legitimacy, varying urgency), environmental groups (high legitimacy, high urgency for ecological impact), property developers (high power, high legitimacy for land use), and city council members (high power, high legitimacy, high urgency for public approval). The model helps determine whose input is most critical at different stages.

## Common Pitfalls and Anti-Patterns
*   **Misjudging Attributes:** Incorrectly assessing a stakeholder's power, legitimacy, or urgency can lead to misprioritization and ineffective engagement. For instance, underestimating the power of a seemingly minor group or overestimating the legitimacy of a vocal but unrepresentative one.
*   **Ignoring Less Salient Stakeholders:** While the model prioritizes, it doesn't suggest ignoring all others. A pitfall is to completely disregard stakeholders with low salience, who might still pose risks or offer opportunities if not managed appropriately.
*   **Bias Towards the Loudest:** The model helps counteract the natural human tendency to prioritize the most vocal or visible stakeholders, ensuring a more objective assessment based on defined criteria rather than mere assertiveness.

## Logic Gate
*   **Applicability Check:**
    *   Does the problem involve managing multiple individuals, groups, or entities with vested interests in an outcome?
    *   Are there conflicting demands, expectations, or potential impacts from these parties?
    *   Is there a need to strategically prioritize engagement, communication, or resource allocation among these parties?
    *   Is the context related to project management, change initiatives, policy development, organizational governance, or any scenario requiring structured stakeholder management?
    *   If the answer to these questions is predominantly "yes," this model is highly applicable.

## Thinking Steps
1.  **Identify Stakeholders:** List all parties affected by or affecting the initiative.
2.  **Assess Power:** Evaluate their ability to influence the outcome.
3.  **Assess Legitimacy:** Evaluate the validity of their relationship or claim to the project.
4.  **Assess Urgency:** Evaluate how critical and time-sensitive their claims are.
5.  **Categorize:** Group stakeholders based on possessing one, two, or all three attributes (e.g., Definitive stakeholders have all three and demand immediate attention).

## State Output

Provide a categorized list of stakeholders indicating their levels of Power, Legitimacy, and Urgency.

## Termination Criteria

Prioritize stakeholder engagement strategies based on their categorized salience level.
