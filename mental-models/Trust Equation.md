## Model ID: TRUST_EQUATION

## Definition and Core Principle
*   The Trust Equation, developed by David Maister, Charles Green, and Robert Galford, posits that trust in a relationship is a function of four variables: Credibility, Reliability, Intimacy, and Self-Orientation.
*   It provides a framework to diagnose and improve trust by identifying which specific component is weakest, emphasizing that reducing self-orientation has the most significant positive impact.

## Concrete Examples and Analogies
*   **Client-Consultant Relationship:** A client might trust a consultant's expertise (Credibility) but question their commitment to the client's best interest (Self-Orientation) if the consultant frequently pushes for additional, expensive services that seem to benefit them more than the client.
*   **Team Dynamics:** A team member might be highly skilled (Credibility) and always deliver on time (Reliability), but if they rarely share personal struggles or vulnerabilities (low Intimacy), or consistently prioritize their own tasks over helping others (high Self-Orientation), overall team trust can suffer.

## Common Pitfalls and Anti-Patterns
*   **Over-simplification:** While a powerful diagnostic tool, it's not a magic formula. Trust is complex and takes time to build; simply "calculating" doesn't instantly fix it.
*   **Ignoring Context:** The relative importance of each variable can shift based on cultural context or the specific nature of the relationship.
*   **Focusing only on the numerator:** People often try to boost credibility, reliability, or intimacy, but overlook that a high self-orientation can negate all those efforts. The model highlights that reducing self-orientation is often the most impactful lever.

## Logic Gate
The LLM should consider applying this model if the problem description involves:
*   **Relationship Dynamics:** The core issue revolves around interpersonal relationships, whether in business (client, partner), internal teams, leadership, or personal contexts.
*   **Trust Deficit:** There are explicit or implicit indications that trust is low, broken, or needs to be strengthened to achieve desired outcomes (e.g., stalled projects, poor collaboration, low sales, high turnover, communication breakdowns).
*   **Diagnostic Need:** The goal is not just to acknowledge a lack of trust, but to systematically understand *why* trust is lacking and identify specific areas for improvement within a relationship.
*   **Focus on Improvement:** The user is seeking actionable insights to build or rebuild trust, rather than just a general discussion about its importance.

## Thinking Steps
1.  **Assess Credibility:** Evaluate the perceived expertise and track record (Words).
2.  **Assess Reliability:** Evaluate the consistency and dependability of actions (Actions).
3.  **Assess Intimacy:** Evaluate the safety and security of sharing information (Emotions).
4.  **Assess Self-Orientation:** Evaluate the perceived focus on self vs. focus on the other party (Motives).
5.  **Calculate:** Recognize that Trust = (Credibility + Reliability + Intimacy) / Self-Orientation. Lowering Self-Orientation has the greatest impact.

## State Output
Diagnose the specific relationship by estimating the four variables and identifying the weakest area.

## Termination Criteria
Develop actionable steps to improve specific components of the equation, particularly focusing on reducing self-orientation.
