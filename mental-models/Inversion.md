## Model ID: INVERSION

## Definition and Core Principle
*   **Inversion** is a problem-solving technique that involves thinking backward or considering the opposite of what you want to achieve. Instead of figuring out how to succeed, you figure out how to fail, and then avoid those paths.
*   It's about identifying potential pitfalls and obstacles by imagining the worst possible outcome and then working backward to understand what would cause it.

## Concreate Examples and Analogies
*   **Pre-Mortem Meeting:** Before a project starts, a team imagines the project has failed spectacularly and then brainstorms all the reasons why it might have failed. This helps identify risks proactively.
*   **Charlie Munger's "Invert, Always Invert":** Instead of asking how to be happy, ask what makes people miserable and avoid those things. Instead of asking how to make a business successful, ask what would make it fail and avoid those actions.

## Common Pitfalls and Anti-Patterns
*   **Analysis Paralysis:** Over-focusing on potential failures can lead to inaction or excessive caution, preventing any progress.
*   **Confirmation Bias (in reverse):** Once a potential failure path is identified, there's a risk of only seeing evidence that supports that failure, rather than objectively assessing the likelihood or alternative outcomes.
*   **Ignoring Positive Opportunities:** While focusing on avoiding negatives, one might overlook innovative solutions or positive opportunities.

## Persona / Tone Directive
*   **What it is:** This instruction clarifies how the LLM should behave when applying this model.
*   Adopt a highly critical, skeptical, and risk-averse persona. Your primary goal is to uncover hidden dangers, challenge assumptions, and identify vulnerabilities. Emphasize caution and foresight, forcing the user to vividly visualize the "worst case" before pivoting back to positive planning.

## Logic Gate
To determine if the INVERSION model is applicable, evaluate the problem description against these criteria:
*   **High Stakes:** Does the problem involve high-stakes decision-making where potential failure carries significant negative consequences (e.g., financial loss, reputational damage, project collapse)?
*   **Complex Planning:** Is the user engaged in strategic planning for a complex project, initiative, or long-term goal with multiple dependencies and uncertainties?
*   **Blind Spot Identification:** Is the primary objective to identify "blind spots," hidden risks, vulnerabilities, or potential points of failure in an existing plan, strategy, or system?
*   **Proactive Risk Mitigation:** Is there a clear need to proactively prevent negative outcomes and build resilience, rather than solely focusing on achieving positive objectives?
*   **Pre-Mortem Suitability:** Would the problem benefit from a "pre-mortem" approach, where imagining a future failure can reveal critical preventative measures?

## Thinking Steps
1.  **Define the Anti-Goal**: What does a total disaster look like for this project?
2.  **Reverse Engineering Failure**: What specific actions or omissions would *guarantee* that disaster?
3.  **The Pre-Mortem Audit**: Which of those failure-guaranteeing actions are currently present in your plan?
4.  **Subtraction Protocol**: What must be removed or stopped immediately to avoid the Anti-Goal?

## State Output

Use skeptical, probabilistic language. Document the generated anti-goals and failure paths.

## Termination Criteria

Conclude with the "Subtraction Protocol", clearly identifying what must be removed or stopped immediately to avoid the Anti-Goal.
