## Model ID: SYSTEMS_THINKING

## Definition and Core Principle
*   Systems Thinking is a holistic approach to understanding how parts of a system interrelate and how systems work over time and within the context of larger systems.
*   Its core principle is that the behavior of the whole system emerges from the interactions and relationships between its components, rather than being a simple sum of its individual parts. It emphasizes identifying feedback loops, delays, and leverage points.

## Concrete Examples and Analogies
*   **Ecosystems**: A forest is not merely a collection of trees, animals, and soil, but a complex system where each element influences the others through nutrient cycles, predator-prey dynamics, and climate regulation. Intervening in one part (e.g., removing a predator) can have cascading, unpredictable effects throughout the entire system.
*   **Healthcare Systems**: Optimizing individual hospital departments (e.g., reducing wait times in the ER) without considering their impact on other departments (e.g., increased demand on inpatient beds, staffing shortages) can shift bottlenecks or create new problems elsewhere in the patient journey.

## Common Pitfalls and Anti-Patterns
*   **Reductionism**: The tendency to break down complex problems into isolated, manageable pieces and optimize each piece independently, often leading to sub-optimization of the whole system or unintended consequences.
*   **Blaming Individuals**: Attributing system failures to the incompetence or malice of individuals, rather than recognizing that individuals often operate within flawed system structures that constrain their choices and drive specific behaviors.

## Logic Gate
*   **Problem exhibits interconnectedness**: Changes or interventions in one area consistently produce unexpected or negative effects in other, seemingly unrelated areas of the problem space.
*   **Organizational or process-level scope**: The challenge involves coordinating, designing, or redesigning elements across multiple teams, departments, or stages of a workflow, where interactions are critical.
*   **Persistent, non-obvious root causes**: Simple linear cause-and-effect analyses (e.g., "5 Whys") fail to resolve recurring issues, suggesting deeper structural or interactive causes within a larger system.
*   **Desire for holistic understanding**: The primary goal is to understand how components interact to produce overall system behavior, rather than isolating and optimizing individual parts.

## Thinking Steps
1.  **Define the System Boundary**: What is "inside" the system you are analyzing and what is the external environment?
2.  **Identify the Components**: List the distinct actors, queues, datastores, or departments involved.
3.  **Map the Interconnections**: How does information, material, or influence flow between these components? Look for feedback loops and delays.
4.  **Identify the System's Purpose**: What is the system *actually* optimized to do (often revealed by its behavior, not its stated goals)?
5.  **Simulate a Change**: If you alter a component or an interconnection, trace the ripple effects through the entire system before implementing it.

## State Output
Maintain a "System Map" detailing components, flows, feedback loops, and boundaries.

## Termination Criteria
Conclude with a "Holistic Intervention Plan," identifying a high-leverage point that improves the entire system without causing destructive downstream ripples.
