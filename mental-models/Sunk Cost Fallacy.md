## Model ID: SUNK_COST_FALLACY

## Definition and Core Principle
*   The Sunk Cost Fallacy is the cognitive bias where individuals continue a behavior or endeavor as a result of previously invested resources (time, money, effort), even when doing so is irrational and goes against their best interests.
*   The core principle is that past costs are unrecoverable ("sunk") and should therefore be irrelevant to future decisions, which should instead be based solely on future costs and future benefits.

## Concrete Examples and Analogies
*   **The Bad Movie**: Continuing to watch a terrible movie at the cinema because you've already paid for the ticket and watched the first hour, rather than leaving to do something more enjoyable.
*   **The Failing Business**: A business owner continuing to invest in a failing product line because of the millions already spent on R&D and marketing, even when market analysis shows no future profitability.

## Common Pitfalls and Anti-Patterns
*   **Confusing Sunk Costs with Future Commitments**: Do not confuse unrecoverable past costs with future contractual obligations or unavoidable expenses that will occur regardless of the decision to continue or stop (e.g., a penalty for breaking a contract). Only truly unrecoverable past investments are sunk costs.
*   **Fear of Admitting Failure**: The model directly counteracts the natural human tendency to avoid admitting a mistake or failure, which can lead to "throwing good money after bad."

## Logic Gate
Apply this model when a decision-making scenario exhibits the following characteristics, indicating a potential Sunk Cost Fallacy:
*   **Presence of Past Investment**: Significant resources (time, money, effort) have already been expended on a course of action, project, or asset.
*   **Decision Point**: A critical choice needs to be made about whether to continue, modify, or abandon the current path.
*   **Justification Based on Past**: There is an explicit or implicit reluctance to change course, often justified by the amount of resources already invested ("we've come too far," "we've spent too much," "it would be a waste").
*   **Focus on Future Viability**: The core problem requires evaluating the *future* viability and returns of the endeavor, independent of its historical performance or investment.
*   **Risk of Escalation**: There's a potential for pouring more resources into a failing venture solely to "recover" or "justify" past investments, rather than based on a sound forward-looking analysis.

## Thinking Steps
1.  **Identify the Sunk Cost**: Explicitly state the time, money, or effort already spent that cannot be recovered.
2.  **The "Zero-Base" Question**: If we were starting fresh today with zero investment and zero history, would we choose to invest in this path based *only* on current information?
3.  **Project Future Costs**: What is the remaining cost (time/money) required to succeed from this exact moment forward?
4.  **Project Future Value**: What is the realistic future payoff if we complete the path?
5.  **The Cold Calculation**: Compare *only* Future Costs against Future Value. If Future Costs > Future Value, the project must be killed, regardless of the Sunk Cost.

## State Output
Log the "Sunk Costs" (to be ignored) and the calculated "Future Costs" vs. "Future Value."

## Termination Criteria
End with a "Forward-Looking Decision," either continuing based on future ROI or immediately abandoning the path.
