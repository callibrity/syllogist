## Model ID: KAIZEN

## Definition & Core Principle
* Kaizen is the Japanese business philosophy of continuous, incremental improvement involving everyone in an organization, from frontline workers to the CEO.
* It rejects massive, disruptive "transformation" projects in favor of daily, micro-optimizations that compound over time into massive gains in quality and efficiency.

## Concrete Examples / Analogies
* **The Toyota Production System:** Instead of waiting for a yearly process review, a factory worker notices a tool is out of reach and moves it 3 inches closer, saving 1 second per cycle.
* **DevOps:** Automating one tiny, manual deployment step every sprint, eventually resulting in a fully automated CI/CD pipeline, rather than halting all feature work for 6 months to "build the pipeline."

## Common Pitfalls / Anti-Patterns
* **Waiting for the "Big Bang":** Ignoring daily friction because leadership is promising a massive "System 2.0" that will fix everything in 18 months.
* **Top-Down Mandates:** Management dictating process improvements to the frontline workers, rather than empowering the people doing the work to identify and solve their own micro-inefficiencies.

## Persona / Tone Directive
* Act as a relentless, pragmatic floor manager. Reject grandiose, multi-year transformation plans and insist on identifying a tiny, annoying problem that can be fixed and standardized by the end of the day.

## Logic Gate
* **Trigger:** The user wants to foster a culture of change, processes feel stagnant, or the team is overwhelmed by the friction of daily operations.
* **Symptom:** The organization is waiting for a massive "silver bullet" project to fix systemic issues, while ignoring the compounding cost of daily, minor inefficiencies.
* **LLM Evaluation Criteria:** IF the scenario involves a desire for process improvement but the proposed solutions are too large, risky, or top-down, THEN apply this model to shrink the scope down to immediate, frontline-driven, incremental fixes.

## Thinking Steps
1. **Identify a Small Problem:** Focus on a minor inefficiency, defect, or safety issue in a daily process.
2. **Involve the Doers:** Engage the people who actually perform the work in finding a solution.
3. **Implement a Small Fix:** Rapidly test a low-cost, low-risk improvement.
4. **Standardize:** If the fix works, update the standard operating procedure.
5. **Repeat:** Make this process a daily or weekly habit.

## State Output

Document the identified problem, the proposed small fix, and the results of the implementation.

## Termination Criteria

Successfully implement and standardize a small, incremental process improvement.
