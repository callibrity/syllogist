## Model ID: PARETO_PRINCIPLE

## Definition and Core Principle
*   The Pareto Principle, also known as the 80/20 rule, posits that for many events, roughly 80% of the effects come from 20% of the causes.
*   Its core principle is to identify the "vital few" inputs or causes that are responsible for the "trivial many" outputs or effects, enabling focused effort for maximum impact.

## Concrete Examples and Analogies
*   **Business Sales**: Approximately 80% of a company's revenue often comes from 20% of its customers.
*   **Software Development**: Roughly 80% of software bugs are typically found in 20% of the code modules.
*   **Personal Productivity**: 80% of your productive results often stem from 20% of your efforts or tasks.

## Common Pitfalls and Anti-Patterns
*   **Rigid Application**: The 80/20 ratio is a guideline, not a strict law. It might be 70/30, 90/10, or another distribution. Don't force the numbers if the data doesn't support it.
*   **Ignoring the "Trivial Many" Entirely**: While the focus is on the vital few, the "trivial many" still contribute and may require attention later or for specific edge cases. Completely neglecting them can lead to other issues.
*   **Misidentifying Inputs/Outputs**: Ensure you are correctly defining and measuring the inputs and outputs relevant to the problem, otherwise, the analysis will be flawed.

## Logic Gate
*   **Condition 1: Imbalance of Impact**: Does the problem description suggest a situation where a small number of factors (inputs) are disproportionately responsible for a large number of outcomes (outputs)?
*   **Condition 2: Prioritization Need**: Is there a clear need to identify the most impactful elements to focus limited resources for maximum effect (e.g., in backlogs, resource allocation, or problem-solving)?
*   **Condition 3: Root Cause or Value Identification**: Is the goal to pinpoint the critical causes of widespread issues or to identify the key drivers of value (e.g., most impactful bugs, most valued features, most profitable clients)?
*   **Directive**: Apply this model when the objective is to identify and prioritize the "vital few" inputs that generate the majority of the desired or undesired outputs, allowing for focused and efficient action.

## Thinking Steps
1.  **Identify the Domain**: Are we looking at bugs, features, clients, or effort?
2.  **Quantify the Outputs**: List the results (e.g., 100 total system crashes, $1M in revenue).
3.  **Map Inputs to Outputs**: Trace the outputs back to their sources (e.g., which specific modules caused the crashes? Which clients generated the revenue?).
4.  **Rank by Impact**: Sort the inputs in descending order of their contribution to the total output.
5.  **The 20% Focus**: Identify the top 20% of inputs that are driving 80% of the results. Ignore the "trivial many" until the "vital few" are addressed.

## State Output
Maintain a ranked list of "Inputs" categorized by their percentage impact on the total "Output."

## Termination Criteria
Conclude with a "Vital Few Action Plan," directing all immediate resources toward the top-yielding 20% of inputs.
