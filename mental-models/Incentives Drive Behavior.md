## Model ID: INCENTIVES_DRIVE_BEHAVIOR

## Definition and Core Principle
*   This model posits that human behavior is primarily driven by the incentives (rewards and punishments, explicit or implicit) present in their environment.
*   Individuals, teams, and organizations will naturally optimize their actions to maximize perceived rewards and minimize perceived costs or punishments, even if these actions are counterproductive to stated goals.

## Concreate Examples and Analogies
*   **Call Center Metrics**: A call center agent rushes through calls, providing inadequate support, because their performance is measured solely on "average handle time" (AHT) rather than customer satisfaction or resolution rate.
*   **Software Development**: Developers prioritize adding new features over fixing bugs because new features are visible and rewarded, while bug fixes are often seen as maintenance and go unacknowledged.

## Common Pitfalls and Anti-Patterns
*   **Blaming Individuals**: The pitfall is to blame individuals for "bad" behavior without examining the systemic incentives that encourage it. This model helps shift focus from individual failings to environmental design.
*   **Ignoring Implicit Incentives**: Overlooking non-monetary incentives like status, recognition, ease of work, or avoiding conflict can lead to misdiagnosis.

## Logic Gate
Apply this model when the problem description includes or strongly implies:
*   **Behavioral Discrepancy**: There's a clear mismatch between desired outcomes and actual human behavior, where people seem to be acting "irrationally" or counter to stated goals.
*   **Systemic Issues**: The problem isn't isolated to one individual but is observed across a team, department, or organization.
*   **Resistance to Change**: New processes, tools, or directives are being ignored or actively circumvented.
*   **Misaligned Goals**: Different parts of an organization appear to be working against each other due to conflicting priorities or reward structures (e.g., sales vs. delivery).
*   **Unintended Consequences**: Current metrics, rewards, or punishments are inadvertently driving undesirable actions.
*   **Core Question**: The central question revolves around *why* people are behaving a certain way, especially when that behavior seems counterproductive or baffling.

## Thinking Steps
1.  **Observe the Behavior**: What is the specific, undesirable action people are consistently taking?
2.  **Assume Rationality**: Stop assuming people are malicious or stupid. Assume they are highly intelligent actors optimizing for their environment.
3.  **Follow the Money/Status**: What is the explicit or implicit reward for this behavior? (e.g., bonuses, promotions, avoiding public reprimand, saving time).
4.  **Identify the Misaligned Incentive**: How does the current reward structure actively punish the *desired* behavior or reward the *undesired* behavior?
5.  **Redesign the Reward**: How can you change the environment so that doing the right thing is the easiest, most rewarding path?

## State Output

Log the "Undesired Behavior" and map it directly to the "Underlying Incentive."

## Termination Criteria

End with an "Incentive Restructuring Proposal," changing the metrics, rewards, or environmental defaults to naturally produce the desired behavior.
