## Model ID: PARKINSONS_LAW

## Definition and Core Principle
*   **Core Principle**: Work expands to fill the time available for its completion. This principle also applies to resources, where demand for resources (budget, personnel, storage) expands to consume the supply.
*   **Implication**: Imposing artificial constraints on time or resources can lead to increased efficiency, focus, and prioritization, preventing unnecessary expansion of effort or consumption.

## Concrete Examples and Analogies
*   **Project Deadlines**: A software development team given six months to complete a project will likely take six months, even if the core functionality could be delivered in three. The extra time is filled with non-essential features, refactoring, or extended testing.
*   **Budget Allocation**: A department allocated a larger budget for the year will almost always find ways to spend the entire amount, often on non-critical items, rather than returning unused funds.
*   **Student Essays**: A student assigned an essay due in a month will often procrastinate and take the full month, even if the actual writing time required is only a few days.

## Common Pitfalls and Anti-Patterns
*   **Over-Constraining**: Applying constraints too aggressively without a realistic understanding of the minimum required effort can lead to burnout, poor quality, or missed deadlines.
*   **Ignoring Complexity**: This model is less effective for genuinely complex tasks that require significant, non-negotiable time or resources. It's best applied where there's clear potential for "filler" work.
*   **Bias Counteracted**: This model directly counteracts the natural human tendency to procrastinate or "gold-plate" when ample time or resources are perceived to be available.

## Logic Gate
*   **Problem Context**: The problem describes a situation where tasks, projects, or resource consumption consistently expand to fill all available time or allocated resources, regardless of actual necessity.
*   **Symptom 1 - Timeline Bloat**: There is evidence that work expands to meet deadlines, leading to perceived inefficiency, procrastination, or unnecessary elaboration.
*   **Symptom 2 - Resource Creep**: Budgets, personnel, storage, or other resources are fully utilized even when expanded, suggesting consumption driven by availability rather than strict need.
*   **Decision Point**: The objective is to proactively enforce artificial constraints (time, budget, resources) to force prioritization, increase efficiency, and prevent work from unnecessarily expanding.

## Thinking Steps
1.  **Identify the Allocation**: What is the current time, budget, or resource allocated to the task?
2.  **Estimate the True Effort**: Ignoring the allocated limit, what is the absolute minimum time/resource required to achieve the "Definition of Done" under ideal conditions?
3.  **Identify the "Filler"**: What non-essential activities, gold-plating, or procrastinations typically fill the gap between true effort and allocated time?
4.  **Apply Artificial Constraints**: Halve the timeline or artificially restrict the resource to force prioritization and focus.
5.  **The Timebox**: Set a strict timebox for the next immediate milestone to create urgency.

## State Output
Log the original allocation, the true effort estimate, and the newly applied artificial constraints.

## Termination Criteria
Conclude with a "Constraint Enforcement Plan," featuring tightened deadlines or resource limits that force efficiency.
