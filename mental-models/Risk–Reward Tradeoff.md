## Model ID: RISK_REWARD

## Definition and Core Principle
*   The Risk-Reward Ratio is a fundamental concept used to evaluate the attractiveness of an investment or decision by comparing the potential profit (reward) to the potential loss (risk).
*   Its core principle is that for any given risk taken, there should be a proportionally greater potential reward to justify undertaking that risk.

## Concrete Examples and Analogies
*   **Stock Trading:** A trader might buy a stock at $50, setting a stop-loss at $45 (risk of $5) and a target price at $65 (reward of $15). This represents a 1:3 risk-reward ratio.
*   **Business Project:** A company considers investing $1M in a new product line. The risk is losing the $1M if it fails. The potential reward is $5M in profit over five years if successful.

## Common Pitfalls and Anti-Patterns
*   **Ignoring Probability:** This model primarily focuses on the *magnitude* of risk and reward, not the *likelihood* of each outcome. A high reward-to-risk ratio is less valuable if the probability of achieving the reward is extremely low.
*   **Underestimating Risk:** Overlooking hidden costs, unforeseen complications, or systemic risks can lead to an inaccurate risk assessment.
*   **Overestimating Reward:** Being overly optimistic about potential returns without sufficient data or conservative projections.

## Logic Gate
*   **Problem Context:** Does the problem involve making a decision about an investment, project, strategic initiative, or any action where resources are committed with an expectation of future gain?
*   **Core Challenge:** Is the primary challenge to weigh potential gains against potential losses?
*   **Decision Criteria:** Is the goal to determine if the expected return or benefit adequately compensates for the inherent risks involved?
*   **Quantification Need:** Does the problem require quantifying or estimating both the upside and downside scenarios?

## Thinking Steps
1.  **Assess Potential Reward:** Quantify the maximum upside of the decision.
2.  **Assess Potential Risk:** Quantify the maximum downside, including total capital loss or opportunity cost.
3.  **Calculate Ratio:** Compare the risk to the reward (e.g., a 1:3 ratio means risking $1 to make $3).
4.  **Evaluate Tolerance:** Determine if the risk level aligns with the organization's or individual's risk tolerance.
5.  **Mitigate Risk:** Identify ways to reduce the downside without proportionally reducing the upside (asymmetric bets).

## State Output
Provide an analysis of the specific risks, potential rewards, the calculated ratio, and mitigation strategies.

## Termination Criteria
Approve or reject the decision based on whether the potential reward adequately compensates for the accepted risk.
