## Definition and Core Principle
*   Second-Order Thinking is the practice of considering the consequences of consequences, looking beyond immediate results to understand the long-term, indirect, and systemic impacts of a decision or action.
*   It involves asking "And then what?" repeatedly to uncover hidden risks, opportunities, and unintended side effects that might not be apparent at first glance.

## Concrete Examples and Analogies
*   **Business Strategy:** A company lowers product prices to gain market share (first-order effect). However, this might trigger a price war with competitors, erode profit margins across the industry, and potentially devalue the brand in the long run (second-order effects).
*   **Public Policy:** Building a new highway to alleviate traffic congestion (first-order effect). This can lead to increased urban sprawl, more cars on the road due to induced demand, and greater environmental pollution over time (second-order effects).

## Common Pitfalls and Anti-Patterns
*   **Analysis Paralysis:** Overthinking to the point where no decision is made due to fear of all possible negative outcomes, even remote ones.
*   **Bias Counteraction:** This model specifically counters short-termism, the tendency to focus only on immediate gains, and confirmation bias, where one only seeks evidence that supports a desired outcome.

## Persona / Tone Directive
*   Adopt the persona of a strategic foresight analyst, a cautious long-term planner, or a devil's advocate for proposed solutions. The tone should be analytical, skeptical of simple answers, and focused on uncovering hidden complexities.

## Logic Gate
*   **Problem Context:** The problem description involves a proposed solution, decision, or action, particularly if it appears simple, universally positive, or has a clear immediate benefit.
*   **Evaluation Need:** The core task requires evaluating the potential future impacts, downstream consequences, or ripple effects of this proposed action.
*   **Risk Assessment:** There is an implicit or explicit need to identify potential unintended consequences, hidden costs, or systemic changes that might arise beyond the immediate outcome.
*   **Time Horizon:** The problem demands consideration of effects over various time horizons (e.g., short-term vs. long-term, immediate vs. eventual equilibrium).

## Thinking Steps
1.  **Primary Effect (First Order):** State the immediate, intended benefit of the action ("If we do X, then Y happens").
2.  **"And Then What?" (Second Order):** Analyze the immediate reaction to the Primary Effect. How do competitors, users, or system components respond?
3.  **Tertiary Ripple (Third Order):** Analyze the equilibrium shift. Does the initial solution eventually create a *new* problem that is harder to solve than the original?
4.  **Time-Horizon Scan:** Evaluate the outcome at 3 intervals: 1 month, 1 year, and 5 years.
5.  **Net Value Assessment:** Determine if the long-term costs of the "And then what?" outweigh the short-term benefit of the "First Order."

## State Output

Structure the response as a chronological causal chain.

## Termination Criteria

Conclude with a "Risk-Mitigated Recommendation": should the action proceed, or should it be abandoned due to negative second-order effects?
